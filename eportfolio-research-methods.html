Reflection

Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI

Unit 1

Topic

Review Corrêa et al. (2023) and discuss global consensus on ethical Artificial Intelligence (AI) policies, recommend course of action and impact on legal, social and professional issues.

Outcomes

  • 1: The Corrêa et al. (2023) dataset concludes in 2022, making it outdated for generative AI's impact on ethical AI. Recent advancements, like the Bletchley declaration and EU AI Act are steps toward consensus and enforcement, respectively (European Parliament, 2023; GOV.UK, 2023). Industrial research in addition to academic shows balance is needed between human-centric and innovation, and interoperability is essential global consensus (Holistic AI, 2024; OECD.AI, 2024). This practical analysis is missing from the academic studies.

Feedback

"Strong academic writing style". Good use of visuals. Recommendations: Formatting requirements, more debate for criticality, do not use colloquial words.

  • 1: Reflection

Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Surveys

Unit 5

Topic

In 2018, Cambridge Analytica misused Facebook user data (Afriat et al., 2020). Exemplify further inappropriate survey use, highlighting ethical, social, legal and professional impacts.

Outcomes

  • 1: Push polls, not only Artificial Intelligence (AI) survey misuse, are also unethical (Murphy et al., 2021). Users still want social media, however, and some seem willing to trade data privacy (Afriat et al., 2020). Research using Yahoo Finance's (N.D.) Nasdaq META (Facebook) stock graph reflected the lack of regulatory and ethical impact succinctly and complemented Wagner's (2021) discussion on penalties and 'privacy by design'.

Feedback

'Well paced and reflects key facts in a direct manner'. Good use of debate 'pros' and 'cons', citation, graphics, layout and paragraph weighting.

  • 5: Reflection

Collaborative 2

Accuracy of Information

Units 7, 8, 9

Topic

Statistical programmer and researcher, Abi discovers data indicating product may be harmful. Evaluate ethical concerns and highlight the legal, social and professional impacts of various choices.

Outcomes

  • 1: Used Holmes et al.'s (2017) and Berenson et al.'s (2019) ethics for statisticians to demonstrate which actions would breach the ACM (2018) code of conduct. The Andrew Wakefield case showed how Abi could be held responsible (Hasnain, 2013).
  • 3: A peer respondent raised Godlee et al.'s (2011) observation of Wakefield's conflict of interest for a pharmaceutical lawsuit. I discerned that Godlee et al.'s (2011) publisher was in conflict of interest for receiving pharmaceutical money, therefore, potentially reducing credibility.

Feedback

Peer response: Andrew Wakefield described as an 'ideal real-world example' but more explicit responses requested. Interestingly, when another student had a different viewpoint on his initial post, I understood how misinterpretation through researcher bias or error can completely change perspective (Saunders et al., 2019).
